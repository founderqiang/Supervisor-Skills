# 2.3 Advanced: How to Pursue "Disruptive Innovation" (Paradigm Shift)

The preceding "higher, faster, stronger" framework belongs to Incremental Innovation — running faster than others on a track that someone else has already defined. This is a highly effective and safe strategy for PhD students getting started and for publishing top-conference papers.

However, truly top-tier scholars — as described by Richard Hamming in his classic talk *You and Your Research* — are not satisfied with this. What they pursue is Disruptive Innovation / Paradigm Shift — not playing by the existing rules, but instead defining a new problem and opening up a completely new track.

For example, when everyone was studying how to use complex CNN architectures and RNN/LSTM to improve machine-translation accuracy, the Google team discarded all these complex recurrent structures and proposed "Attention Is All You Need" (Transformer). This is a typical example of disruptive innovation.

How do you cultivate the ability to open up such ground-breaking problems? Although there is no fixed formula, you can start from the following thinking paradigms:

## 1. First Principles Thinking

Do not start from the existing literature — start from the physical or mathematical essence of the problem. Existing solutions are often built upon certain historical assumptions that may no longer hold.

- **Break implicit assumptions**: Scrutinize the "common sense" acknowledged in the current field. For example, traditional database query optimization has long assumed that "the data distribution and workload are static, and the query plan must be fully determined before execution". If you break this assumption and hold that "the query plan can be changed dynamically during execution based on the observed data", you give rise to the entirely new direction of Adaptive Query Processing.
- **Go back to the original purpose**: Ask yourself "what is this task ultimately for?" rather than "how do others do it?" In the Agent era, take data cleaning as an example. The traditional approach is to write a bunch of rule scripts that clean data independently, e.g., imputing missing values, de-noising. If you go back to first principles, the ultimate purpose of data cleaning is to make downstream tasks, e.g., analysis or modeling, work better. Then, why not let an LLM Agent directly and autonomously write code and iteratively refine the cleaning strategy based on feedback from the downstream task? This gives rise to the new direction of Agent-driven Data Cleaning.

## 2. Finding "The Elephant in the Room"

In every research field, there are some "tough bones" or "pain points" that everyone knows about but deliberately avoids. These problems are often collectively ignored by the research community because they are too hard to solve, or not suitable for writing papers.

- **Face real-world pain points head-on**: Academia likes to rank on clean Benchmarks, but real industry may face completely different challenges. For example, academia has long focused on the accuracy of Text-to-SQL on single tables or perfect Schemas, but industry's real pain point is that "there are thousands upon thousands of tables in the database, and even the DBA does not know which one to use". If you can formalize this pain point as a new academic problem, e.g., large-scale Schema Linking and retrieval, you are opening up a very valuable new direction.
- **Pay attention to the failing corner cases**: Where does the current mainstream paradigm completely fail? For example, current monolithic LLM Agents perform astonishingly well on simple tasks, but when facing long-horizon, multi-modal, cross-departmental real business tasks, e.g., automatically developing and deploying a complete Web application, they often fall into infinite loops or hallucinations. Grasping this fundamental flaw — rather than doing patch-and-fix Prompt Engineering — and proposing Multi-Agent Collaboration frameworks or SOP-based (Standard Operating Procedure) Agentic Workflow: this is opening up a new research paradigm.

## 3. Technology Cycle Foresight Across Technology Cycles

Disruptive innovation often occurs when there is a generational shift in the underlying hardware or fundamental technology.

- **Embrace new hardware/new media**: When new hardware such as non-volatile memory (NVM), RDMA networks, or GPUs appears, do not think about "how to port old database algorithms to run on new hardware". Instead, think about "if storage is persistent and byte-addressable, does the overall database architecture still need a Buffer Pool? Does it still need traditional Write-Ahead Logging?" This kind of thinking directly gives rise to database systems with brand-new architectures.
- **Assume "infinite resources"**: Do a thought experiment — if computational power or storage space suddenly increased by ten-thousand-fold, and cost is almost zero, what fundamental changes would occur in the way we currently solve problems? For example, if compute is infinite, do we still need to carefully design features? The eruption of deep learning is precisely based on this release of the compute dividend. Today, if LLM Token cost drops to zero and the Context Window is infinitely large, what will the paradigm of data processing become?

## 4. Build Your "Important Problem List" (The Hamming's Rule)

Richard Hamming once made a very famous suggestion: every scientist should keep 10 to 20 of the most important problems in their field in their head at all times.

- **Stay sensitive and be a Prepared Mind**: These important problems may currently have no clue at all for a solution, but you must keep them in your head. When you read papers from other fields, hear about a new algorithm, or see a new technological breakthrough, you have to immediately ask yourself, as a conditioned reflex: "Can this new thing be used to solve some important problem on my list?"
- **Dare to invest in important problems**: If you only ever do those "sure-to-succeed" side-corner problems, you will never be able to make disruptive contributions. You must dare to devote a portion of your energy to gnawing on those big problems that may fail, but once succeeded, can reshape the landscape of the field.

## Takeways

"Higher, faster, stronger" teaches you how to write an excellent paper that can be accepted by a top conference, while "disruptive innovation" determines whether you can become a researcher whose name is written into textbooks. During your PhD career, it is recommended to treat "incremental innovation" as the safety net and the cornerstone for training research taste, and at the same time always maintain a keen sense of smell for, and a courageous pursuit of, "disruptive innovation".
