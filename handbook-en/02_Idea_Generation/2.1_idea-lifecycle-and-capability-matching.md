# 2.1 The Lifecycle of an Idea and Matching with Capabilities

## The Lifecycle of an Idea

Every paper's Idea has its own lifecycle. For example, in the currently hot field of LLM Agents, if the research direction you choose is an application-oriented task based on LLM Agents — such as an application in a specific industry — then the lifecycle of your Idea may be only 3 to 6 months. This means that if you cannot finish coding, experiment design, and paper writing and submit in time within this time frame, then this Idea is very likely to become outdated, eventually leading to a paper that is difficult to publish and causing your earlier investment to go to waste.

Therefore, if your ability is solid, never procrastinate. You must actively discuss with your advisor, ensure that you iterate quickly and implement quickly during the research process, and submit as early as possible. Especially in such a fast-developing field, time is often the decisive factor: the earlier you release your research outcomes, the better you can avoid being preempted by others.

> Note: "Fast" does not equal "low quality". Fast iteration and submission mean that you have to work efficiently, but you still need to maintain a high level of research quality, ensuring that experimental design is sound, results are persuasive, and the paper's content is complete and in-depth.

## Evaluating Topic Feasibility from the Lifecycle of an Idea

By now, you should understand that the lifecycle of an Idea is like an hourglass: it has a clear time limit. Based on this lifecycle, you need to consider how to choose a suitable topic. If you choose a hot Idea, then you must ensure that your capabilities in coding, experimental design, and paper writing are strong enough to finish all the necessary work in a short time. Competition in hot fields is fierce and technology updates rapidly, so time management and execution are especially important.

## Evaluating the Match Between an Idea and a Student's Capability Profile

When choosing a research Idea, you need to evaluate which types of Ideas are most suitable for you based on your current skill stack, the time you are willing to invest, and how solid your fundamentals are.

- If your effective working time per day is less than 5 hours, and your effective working time per week is less than 50 hours, then unless you have very strong learning and execution abilities, I feel it is somewhat difficult to publish in top conferences and journals.
- To help everyone choose a research direction more rationally according to their own circumstances, I list a table below for reference. It can help you better evaluate which Ideas are suitable for you based on your own characteristics, thereby improving the likelihood of success.

| Idea Type | Lifecycle | Suitable Student Profile | Description |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Application-oriented research | Short-term (3–6 months) | Students with solid coding skills, fast execution, and the ability to quickly implement experiments | These students need to be able to code, debug, and conduct experiments quickly, and write papers in a short time. Usually suitable for technically strong research topics with fast application demands. |
| Fundamental theoretical research | Long-term (6–12 months) | Students with a solid theoretical foundation and the ability to think deeply | Suitable for students who have strong mathematical and theoretical foundations and can carry out relatively long-term reasoning, argumentation, and model construction. Requires deep thinking and sustained effort. |
| Interdisciplinary research | Medium-term (6–9 months) | Students transitioning into computer science from other disciplines | Such students may have an interdisciplinary perspective, suitable for research projects that require knowledge from different fields, especially at the intersection of technology and the humanities. Suitable for submitting to HCI-area journals or conferences (CHI, etc.). |
| Frontier-exploration research | Short- to medium-term (3–9 months) | Students with both theoretical and experimental capabilities and strong self-learning ability | Suitable for students who can both run quick experiments and conduct deep theoretical analysis, especially in research fields involving the latest technologies or rapid development. Requires rapid adaptation to change. |
| Data-intensive research | Medium-term (6–12 months) | Students with strong data-analysis ability, solid coding, and the ability to handle large data volumes | Suitable for students skilled in data analysis, algorithms, and model implementation. Requires good data-processing ability and the capability to iterate experiments and model designs quickly. |
| Innovative technology research | Long-term (12 months or more) | Students with solid fundamentals who are willing to challenge existing theories and technologies | Suitable for students with deep academic foundations who can carry out cross-domain innovation. Requires strong logical thinking and experimental-design ability, and the capability to independently push forward relatively long-cycle projects. |
