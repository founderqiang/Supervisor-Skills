# Paper Writing — Thinking Template for Benchmark/Evaluation Papers (for Discussion)

> **Design intent**: When discussing a Benchmark/Evaluation paper project during idea brainstorming, progress reports, or the writing stage, you can use the tables in this template to instantiate your thoughts and facilitate discussion. This template is based on the DIAL Lab's thinking model for Technical Full Papers, and has been specifically optimized by incorporating the structural characteristics of Benchmark papers recently accepted at top venues such as NeurIPS and ICLR (StatQA [NeurIPS 2024], nvBench 2.0 [NeurIPS 2025], VisJudge-Bench [ICLR 2026]).

---

## 1. Quick Positioning: Core Differences Between Benchmark/Evaluation Papers and Technique Papers

Before starting to write, it is necessary to first clarify the fundamental differences between Benchmark papers and Technique Papers in terms of their narrative backbones.

| Dimension | Technique Paper | Benchmark/Evaluation Paper |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Core contribution** | New method / new algorithm | New evaluation dimension / evaluation system / dataset |
| **Introduction backbone** | Key Idea / Mechanism | **Evaluation Gap + Our Benchmark Design** |
| **Role of "problem definition"** | Goal can be stated in one sentence | The problem definition itself is a contribution (defines "what to evaluate" and "how to evaluate") |
| **Method chapter** | The core of the paper (largest portion) | The dataset construction Pipeline is the core; a companion method is a bonus |
| **Purpose of experiments** | To prove "my method is better than others" | To reveal "where the capability boundaries of existing models lie" and to provide deep insights |
| **Most important figures/tables** | Method framework diagram | Benchmark comparison table + Construction Pipeline figure + multi-dimensional analysis figures |

The core of a Benchmark paper lies not only in "proposing a dataset", but more importantly in **defining a new evaluation dimension or problem, and providing a systematic measurement standard and infrastructure**.

> What exactly is the Gap, and why is this Benchmark or this Evaluation paper needed? Articulating the Gap and Motivation clearly is extremely important.

## 2. The Five Core Elements of a Benchmark Paper

An excellent Benchmark paper typically contains the following five core elements. During the ideation stage, you should make sure that each element has a clear answer.

**Element 1: A clear Research Gap (evaluation blind spot).** Existing models or evaluation systems have a blind spot on a key capability. For example, StatQA points out that existing mathematical reasoning Benchmarks focus only on computational results while ignoring the evaluation of the applicability of statistical methods; nvBench 2.0 points out that existing Text2VIS Benchmarks all follow the single-correct-answer paradigm while ignoring query ambiguity; VisJudge-Bench points out that existing evaluations focus only on surface-level aesthetics or information understanding, while ignoring the comprehensive evaluation of Fidelity-Expressiveness-Aesthetics.

**Element 2: A systematic construction methodology (Construction Pipeline).** How to construct a dataset in a high-quality, scalable, and low-cost way. All three papers adopt innovative automated or semi-automated construction pipelines. StatQA adopts "reverse synthesis" (first set the answer, then generate the question); nvBench 2.0 adopts "controllable ambiguity injection" (starting from an unambiguous seed and injecting ambiguity); VisJudge-Bench adopts "adaptive question generation + three-stage expert annotation".

**Element 3: A multi-dimensional evaluation framework (Evaluation Framework).** Not only overall metrics, but also fine-grained taxonomies, capable of diagnosing the capability boundaries of models along different sub-dimensions. For example, StatQA divides statistical tasks into 5 categories and sets Easy/Hard difficulty levels; nvBench 2.0 defines 4 ambiguity types and 5 ambiguity levels; VisJudge-Bench constructs 6 measurable sub-dimensions under the three major dimensions of "Fidelity, Expressiveness, Aesthetics" ("Xin-Da-Ya").

**Element 4: Deep empirical insights (Empirical Findings).** Experiments are not merely about running scores (Leaderboard); more importantly, through multi-angle comparisons (Human vs. LLM, different model architectures, different error type distributions), they should reveal the intrinsic flaws of models and point the way for future research. All three papers distilled explicit Findings from their experiments and discussed Research Opportunities.

**Element 5: A companion validation method (Optional but Highly Recommended).** Besides evaluation, if you can propose a specialized optimization model based on this Benchmark and demonstrate that it can significantly improve the capability in question, it will greatly enhance the depth and value of the paper. nvBench 2.0 proposes Step-Text2Vis (based on Step-DPO preference optimization); VisJudge-Bench proposes VisJudge (based on GRPO reinforcement learning fine-tuning).

## 3. Flowchart for Writing/Conceiving the Introduction (Benchmark-Specific Version)

The Introduction of a Benchmark paper can be viewed as a "compressed version" of the whole paper. Unlike a Technique Paper, the backbone of a Benchmark paper's Introduction is not the "Key Idea / Mechanism" but rather **"Evaluation Gap + Benchmark Design Rationale"**. It is recommended to unfold along the following logical chain.

### 3.1 Flowchart Overview

```
Research Background → Running Example → Limitations of Existing Benchmarks → Research Gap/Questions
    → Design Considerations → Our Proposal → Contributions
```

### 3.2 Detailed Description of Each Stage

| Logical Stage | Core Content and Goal | Writing Tips | Reference Examples |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **1. Research background and core scenario** | Introduce the research object and explain the importance of the task in the real world. | Use a carefully designed **Running Example (Figure 1)** to intuitively illustrate the complexity of the task and how human experts handle it. Figure 1 is one of the most important figures in the whole paper, and must be carefully polished. | StatQA: complete flowchart of a statistical analysis task; nvBench 2.0: multiple valid interpretations of an ambiguous query; VisJudge-Bench: positive vs. negative examples of Fidelity/Expressiveness/Aesthetics. |
| **2. Limitations of existing Benchmarks (The Gap)** | Summarize the blind spots of existing Benchmarks or evaluation systems, typically no more than 3 points. | Emphasize "what key dimensions existing work has overlooked". **It is strongly recommended to provide a Benchmark comparison table in the Introduction or Related Work**, comparing feature dimensions item by item, so that the Gap is at a glance. | nvBench 2.0: Table 1 compares 7 Benchmarks across 6 dimensions; VisJudge-Bench: Table 1 compares 7 Benchmarks across 3 evaluation dimensions. |
| **3. Core research questions (Research Questions)** | Translate the Gap into 2-3 concrete research questions. | The RQs should cover: (1) How to construct an appropriate Benchmark? (2) Where are the capability boundaries of existing models? (3) What are the gaps/differences between models and human experts? | StatQA: Q1 how to evaluate? Q2 how capable are LLMs? Q3 Human vs LLM? |
| **4. Design Considerations** | Make clear what design requirements the Benchmark needs to satisfy. | This is a paragraph unique to Benchmark papers. Before formally introducing the proposal, first articulate "what properties a good Benchmark should have", providing a rationale for subsequent design. | nvBench 2.0: "This benchmark should include diverse ambiguous queries, multiple valid outputs, reasoning paths, and broad domain coverage." |
| **5. Our Proposal** | Formally introduce the Benchmark proposed in this paper and its core characteristics. | Emphasize the scale, diversity of the Benchmark, and most importantly, the core construction innovation. Summarize the core idea of the Pipeline in one or two sentences. | StatQA: "Our key idea is to reverse this process by synthesizing the question Q based on target answers A." |
| **6. Wrapping up the contributions (Contributions)** | Summarize the 3-4 core contributions of the whole paper. | Typical structure: (1) the Benchmark itself (scale, characteristics); (2) innovation of the construction Pipeline; (3) systematic evaluation and deep Findings; (4) companion optimization model (if any). Each contribution should be followed by the corresponding section number. | All three papers adopt this structure. |

### 3.3 Key Figures and Tables in the Introduction

| Element | Function | Suggested Position |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Figure 1: Running Example** | Use a concrete example to intuitively show the complexity of the task and the necessity of the evaluation. | Top of page 1 or page 2. |
| **Table 1: Benchmark comparison table** | Item-by-item comparison of existing Benchmarks across feature dimensions, highlighting the differentiating advantages of this work. | End of the Introduction or in the Related Work. |

## 4. Main Chapter Structure Planning

Combining the common paradigms of the three top-venue papers, the following chapter structure is recommended to organize the whole paper.

### Section 2: The Proposed Benchmark (main body of the dataset, about 3-4 pages)

This is the core chapter of a Benchmark paper, equivalent to the method chapter of a Technique Paper.

**2.1 Design Goals & Task Scope.** Clarify the design goals of constructing this Benchmark as well as the task boundaries. Design goals typically include: (G1) high coverage / representativeness; (G2) support for fine-grained evaluation; (G3) low human cost / scalability; (G4) high data quality. The task boundaries need to clearly delineate "what to evaluate" and "what not to evaluate".

**2.2 Benchmark Construction Pipeline.** This is the most core technical chapter. Describe in detail how the data is constructed step by step. **A clear Pipeline flowchart must be provided (typically Figure 2)**. The Pipeline typically contains the following stages:

| Stage | Description | Writing Tips |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Data collection / preprocessing | Where does the raw data come from? How is it filtered and cleaned? | Explain the diversity and authenticity of data sources. |
| Core synthesis / transformation logic | The innovative core of the Pipeline. How are Benchmark samples generated from raw data? | This is the part reviewers pay most attention to. You need to describe in detail the input, output, and key operations of each step. |
| Quality control and validation | How is data quality ensured? | Typically includes: automated verification + human expert review. Inter-annotator agreement (e.g., Cohen's Kappa, ICC) needs to be reported. |

**2.3 Benchmark Characteristics.** Use rich figures and tables to present the statistical features of the dataset.

| Content to present | Recommended figure/table type |
| --- | --- |
| Task / type distribution | Pie chart, ring chart, bar chart |
| Difficulty / ambiguity level distribution | Bar chart |
| Data scale statistics | Table (rows, columns, sample sizes, etc.) |
| Comparison with existing Benchmarks | Feature comparison table (Table 1) |
| Data examples | Fully annotated example figure |

### Section 3: Specialized Model / Methodology (optional, about 1-2 pages)

If the paper proposes not only a Benchmark but also an optimization method for the problem, introduce it here. Explain how to use the newly constructed Benchmark data (such as Step-wise reasoning paths, fine-grained annotations, preference data) to fine-tune or optimize existing models. The presence of this chapter can significantly enhance the depth of the paper, because it answers "this Benchmark not only enables evaluation, but also helps improve model capabilities".

### Section 4: Experiments & Empirical Findings (Experiments and Insights, about 4-5 pages)

The experiments chapter is another major highlight of a Benchmark paper. Unlike a Technique Paper, the goal of experiments in a Benchmark paper is not "to prove that my method is the best", but rather **"to reveal the capability boundaries of existing models, and to provide deep insights"**.

**4.1 Experimental Setup.** The following elements need to be made clear:

| Element | Description |
| --- | --- |
| Evaluation protocols (Protocols) | How does the model receive input? How is the output extracted and evaluated? |
| Evaluation metrics (Metrics) | Which metrics are chosen? Why? (e.g., Accuracy, F1@K, MAE, Correlation) |
| Baseline models | Cover representative models that are open-source vs. closed-source, of different scales, and from different families. |
| Prompting strategies | 0-shot, few-shot, CoT, domain knowledge prompting, etc. |
| Human baseline experiments | How are they designed? Participant backgrounds? Experimental protocol? |

**4.2 Overall Performance.** Present a large table (typically the largest table in the whole paper), covering the performance of all models on all metrics. The table should use colors or bold fonts to mark the best/second-best results.

**4.3 Fine-grained Analysis (organized by Research Questions).** This is the essence of the experiments chapter of a Benchmark paper. It is recommended to organize sub-sections by Research Questions or analysis dimensions.

| Analysis dimension | Analysis content | Recommended visualization |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Multi-dimensional capability dissection | Performance differences of models across different sub-tasks / difficulty levels | Radar chart, grouped bar chart |
| Error type analysis (Error Taxonomy) | Define an error taxonomy and analyze the distribution of error types the model makes | Stacked bar chart, pie chart |
| Model behavioral bias | Whether the model has systematic biases (e.g., score inflation, overly conservative) | Distribution density curves |
| Strategy impact analysis | The effects of different prompting strategies / fine-tuning methods | Comparison table |
| Human vs. LLM comparison | Differences between human experts and models in terms of strengths/weaknesses | Radar chart, comparative bar chart |

**4.4 Case Studies.** Select 2-4 representative examples to qualitatively illustrate the model's successes and failures in specific scenarios. Case Studies allow reviewers to intuitively understand the value of the Benchmark and the specific flaws of the models.

**Important writing technique: Finding summaries.** After each important analysis paragraph, distill and display in bold a summary sentence of the form **Finding X: ...**. This is a signature writing style of Benchmark papers, making it easy for reviewers to quickly grasp the core conclusions. For example:

> **Finding 1.** Few-shot learning and the inclusion of domain knowledge are helpful for LLMs in this task, whereas CoT is more likely to result in slight performance degradation in smaller models.

### Section 5: Discussion & Research Opportunities (Discussion and Outlook, about 1 page)

Based on the above Findings, discuss future research directions in the field. The presence of this chapter reflects the "forward-looking" value of the Benchmark paper. Typical discussion directions include: how to enhance the core capabilities of models on this task, Human-AI collaboration modes, and future extension directions of the Benchmark.

### Section 6: Related Work (about 1 page)

Systematically review related work. **Be sure to provide a comparison table**, clearly showing the differences and advantages of the proposed Benchmark compared to existing Benchmarks across various dimensions. The position of Related Work can be flexibly adjusted: either placed in Section 2 (like VisJudge-Bench) or placed in the second-to-last section (like StatQA, nvBench 2.0).

### Section 7: Conclusion (about 0.5 page)

Briefly summarize the core contributions and key findings of the whole paper.

## 6. Benchmark Paper Writing Checklist

Before submission, please check the following points one by one.

### Introduction Checklist

- [ ] Is there a carefully designed Running Example (Figure 1)?

- [ ] Are the key blind spots of existing Benchmarks clearly pointed out (no more than 3 points)?

- [ ] Is a Benchmark comparison table (Table 1) provided?

- [ ] Are 2-3 Research Questions clearly listed?

- [ ] Are the Design Considerations articulated?

- [ ] Do the contribution points correspond to the RQs and the chapter structure?

### Benchmark Chapter Checklist

- [ ] Are there clear Design Goals?

- [ ] Is there a complete Pipeline flowchart (Figure 2)?

- [ ] Does each step of the Pipeline have clear descriptions of input, output, and operations?

- [ ] Is the quality control strategy described in detail?

- [ ] Are rich dataset statistical features shown (figures/tables)?

- [ ] Are data examples (Figure) provided?

### Experiments Chapter Checklist

- [ ] Do the Baseline models cover representative models that are open-source/closed-source, of different scales?

- [ ] Is there an Overall Performance large table?

- [ ] Is there multi-dimensional Fine-grained Analysis?

- [ ] Is there an Error Taxonomy or behavioral bias analysis?

- [ ] Is there a Human vs. LLM comparison experiment? (Depending on the task type, Human performance may not be required)

- [ ] Are there Case Studies?

- [ ] Are **Finding X** summaries distilled after key analyses?

- [ ] Are Research Opportunities discussed?

### Overall Checklist

- [ ] Does the whole paper have a clear logical main line? (Gap → Benchmark → Evaluation → Insights → Opportunities)

- [ ] Are the code and data open-sourced, and is the link provided in the paper (in the Abstract or the Contributions summary paragraph of the Intro)?

- [ ] Is there a discussion of Limitations and Future Work?

- [ ] Does the Appendix contain sufficient supplementary material (more examples, full experimental results, annotation guidelines, etc.)?
