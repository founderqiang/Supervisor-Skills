# 3.5 Writing Details and Checklist

The most important thing for a paper is the topic, followed by novelty and contribution, and then experimental results. Only when these three aspects are all in place can you enter the stage of writing the paper.

## Why Is Choosing the Topic the Most Important? A Good Question Is the Starting Point of Good Research

- The best kind of question opens up a new field and drives the development of an industry, such as Nobel Prizes and Turing Awards.
- The four Turing Awards in the database field: the father of databases, the father of relational databases, the father of transaction theory, and the father of database systems.
- The second-best kind of question ignites a wave of excitement and receives wide adoption, such as Spark and vLLM.
- The third-best kind of question becomes a research hotspot and is heavily cited. In the DB field, more than 100 citations is already decent research (don't compare with AI; different fields, apples and oranges), more than 1000 is rare, and more than 10000 is once in a decade.
- I'll take time later to talk specifically about how to choose a topic.

## DB Has Very High Requirements for Novelty and Contribution

You must give reviewers a sense of being impressed at first sight. More importantly, you should let readers learn something and feel that the paper has substance. How do we evaluate whether the novelty and contribution are high?

- If the sub-questions/methods of this problem can be thought of within ten minutes, that means the novelty is insufficient, and it's not enough.
- If a close peer can see it / not see it at a glance, that means the novelty is sufficient.

## Experimental Results Are an Important Evaluation That Validates the Effectiveness of the Proposed Method or System

- If the experimental results are not good, the paper may still have value—showing that the problem is hard—but it is not a good research work.
- DB has high requirements for experimental results: performance improvement of at least 50%, and accuracy/precision improvement of 10% or more is ideal.

## DB Has Extremely High Writing Requirements for Papers

Only after satisfying the three conditions of a novel topic, strong novelty, and good experimental results can excellent writing get the paper accepted. So, how can we write papers well? The following provides a detailed checklist; here is a highly condensed summary, similar to the Abstract of a paper.

- **Logic is the most critical**: The logic of the paper must be clear, so that readers follow the paper's line of thinking and do not raise other doubts.
- **Outline first, details later**: The paper should first introduce the whole, then the details, so that readers grasp the global picture first and then see the details. Whether it's the Framework (the overall introduction of the whole paper), or the first section of each chapter, always introduce the overall picture first and then unfold the details.
- **Paragraphs should be connected**
- **Each paragraph should have a leading text, and the whole paragraph should revolve around one topic**
- **Sentences should be connected**
- **Well-illustrated with figures**: A good system architecture diagram plays a crucial role. If the figure is not drawn clearly, it means the author has not thought clearly, and cannot possibly write clearly either. It's like studying a course—if you can't draw its core content, it means you haven't understood it at all.
- **Example running through the whole paper**: Sometimes a principle is hard to understand, but through an example that intuitively expresses the underlying principle, readers can understand it more easily.
- **Self-contained**: The paper should be written clearly so that readers can understand its content without relying on knowledge from other papers.
- **The paper must be focused**, not scattered; do not include unrelated content.

## English Grammar Issues

On Overleaf, you can install grammar-checking plugins, such as Grammarly and Overleaf's built-in grammar checking plugin, to avoid spelling mistakes and simple third-person singular issues.

### Accurate Word Usage

- Capitalization
- the / a / an
- Singular / plural

### Third-Person Singular, Articles

A sentence cannot have two verbs and cannot have two clauses.

Word choice should be idiomatic and reasonable. Don't just look up a word in the dictionary and throw it in. You can be conservative; symbols should be concise and clear; once defined, they should be used, and don't get confused while using them. **Do not write a Chinese first draft and then use GPT to translate it into English!!!** This will produce many inauthentic or non-standard terms and words!!! Remember, when reading Papers, do not use translation software to translate them into Chinese for reading!!! Both reading and writing should use English!!! Develop good habits.

When you can't add a conjunction between two sentences, be careful.

Take your instructor's / senior students' guidance to heart, and don't keep repeating the same mistakes.

Logic still comes first. Never make people guess the logic. Streamline content, be specific. For long and complex sentences, you can often use "Specifically" to break up the content you want to express.

Use more simple sentences and fewer complex sentences. One sentence should clearly state one thing.

Just introduce things clearly; it's not a show-off of English.

### Grammar Issue: Third-Person Singular

- it predicts, it proposes, we propose, they ...

### Articles: Singular Countable Nouns Must Have Articles

- propose (**a**) novel method

### Simple Sentences, Connected by Conjunctions or Periods

- ❌ we propose a method has high efficiency.
- ✅ we propose a method **that** has high efficiency.
- ✅ we propose a method **with** high efficiency.
- ✅ we propose a high-efficient method.

### Other Common Grammar Issues

- Given a set of queries, we first ... We then ... Moreover, ...
- in (**a**) descending order
- Note that our model supports the case (**that**) ... changed, because ...
- graph embedding **based** database partition
- rather than using ...

## LaTeX Writing Issues

- Chinese and English brackets in documents: be careful to distinguish between Chinese and English brackets.
- Double-quote issue in LaTeX: use ` `` ` and `''` instead of `"`.
- In LaTeX, all file names and folder names, and the labels of internally referenced figures and tables, should not contain spaces, nor should they use hyphens as connectors; they should all use underscores. In addition, use prefixes to improve readability:
  - `\label{fig:system_overview}` ✅
  - `\label{sec:intro}` ✅
  - `\label{system overview}` ❌
- Images should use vector formats such as pdf and svg; otherwise, when using png or jpg, enlarging them will make them blurry.
- Unless there are special cases, figures and tables in the paper are generally placed at the top! If it is really not appropriate, you can consider bottom placement with `[b!]`.
- When citing a paper `~\cite{}` or referring to an internal paragraph or figure `~\ref{}`, use `~` as the connector.
- Please use `ResNet~\cite{xxxx}`, not `ResNet\cite{xxxx}` or `ResNet \cite{xxxx}`, to prevent incorrect line breaks.
- When citing multiple papers, use `Artificial Intelligence~\cite{xxxx, yyyy, zzzz}`.
- Note that citation rules need to be modified according to the specific submission venue requirements.
- Use ` `` ` instead of `"` for double quotes, and similarly for single quotes.

## LLM Writing Issues

> **It is strictly forbidden to use LLMs or AI to generate content; this is absolutely not allowed!** Never do this!

### Cases Where "Hyphens/Em-Dashes Are Forbidden"

#### Connective Use (Semantic Em-Dash)

It is not allowed to use em-dashes to connect two clauses, insert supplementary explanations, or extend content.

- ❌ Example: ... closes the research loop—from literature analysis to deployment.
- ✅ Revised: ... closes the research loop, covering literature analysis, hypothesis generation, simulation, validation, and deployment.

#### Semantic Pause or Emphasis

Avoid using em-dashes as sentence pauses or emphasis markers.

- ❌ Example: This approach—though simple—is highly effective.
- ✅ Revised: Although simple, this approach is highly effective.

### Cases Where "Hyphens Can Be Used"

- ✅ Correct: This approach is called "data-centric AI", which emphasizes the quality of data rather than the model.
- ✅ Correct: The system outputs what it calls "semantic summaries".
- ❌ Common LLM mistake: This approach is called "data-centric AI," which emphasizes the quality of data rather than the model.
- ❌ Common LLM mistake: The system outputs what it calls "semantic summaries."

Explanation: In the logical punctuation system (more common in British English or academic writing), whether punctuation goes inside the quotes depends on semantics; whereas LLMs mostly default to the American style (punctuation always inside the quotes), leading to semantic inconsistencies.

### Avoid the Following LLM-Preferred Vocabulary

Experienced reviewers will be disgusted when they see them.

- **Novelty descriptions**: innovative, pioneering, revolutionary paradigm, transformative framework
- **Performance comparisons**: superior, surpass, excel, remarkable, unprecedented, achieves SOTA, breakthrough performance
- **Contribution summaries**: general-purpose, is capable of
- **Logical structure words**: notably, yet, yielding, at its essence
- **Verbs**: encompass, differentiate, reveal, underscore, surpass, show great potential, exhibit superior capability, exceed, pave the way for, highlight the potential of...
- **Collocations**: profound challenges (no such collocation), stems from
- **Adjectives**: rigid
- **Verbs**: impede

## Fill the Body of the Paper

The body of the paper should make full use of the page space to ensure the content is substantial. Avoid large blank spaces or content that is too sparse. Every page should contain valuable information, letting reviewers feel that the work is solid and complete.

## Writing Guide for Each Section

### Abstract — The High-Level Summary of the Paper, the Core Part of the Article

| Sentence | Content | Key Points |
|---|---|---|
| **First sentence** | What important problem does this paper solve? | what (what problem is solved) + background and significance |
| **Second sentence** | Why solve it? | why: no one has done it / problems with existing methods (new problem or the old problem solved faster, better, more general) |
| **Third sentence** | What are the Challenges? | Challenges must be condensed to be convincing; don't get into minor details; challenges should be related but non-overlapping; challenges are the **challenges of the problem**, not the challenges of the method |
| **Fourth sentence** | How to solve it? | how: what method is proposed? Including several important parts? Should correspond to the challenges |
| **Fifth sentence** | How effective are the experiments? | Must have real datasets; must be specific; must have a code link |

---

### Introduction — Expanded Version of the Abstract, the Most Core Part of the Paper

| Paragraph | Content | Writing Tips |
|---|---|---|
| **First paragraph** | What important problem does this paper solve, give a concrete application example | What problem is solved, what applications |
| **Second paragraph** | Why does it need to be solved? What are the problems with existing methods? | Highly condense the Limitations of existing methods, hit the key points, **do not list too many details**; for new problems, explain application value; for old problems, explain fundamental flaws of existing methods |
| **Third paragraph** | What are the Challenges? Why is it hard? | Write the **challenges of the problem**, not the challenges of the method; be understandable; the challenges should have clear logical relationships, without covering each other |
| **Fourth paragraph** | How to solve it? Give a method summary | The method and challenges should correspond; every claimed advantage should be delivered; **must have novelty, do not write content without novelty** |
| **Fifth paragraph** | Experimental results | Outperform existing methods, with concrete numbers |
| **Sixth paragraph** | Summarize contributions: 3 innovation points + experimental results | Contributions and challenges must correspond! |

**Notes**:
- **Attract reviewers; this almost determines whether the paper can be accepted**
- **Highlight novelty, highlight technical depth**
- **Clearly state the differences from existing methods**
- **Only write about novelty; write little or nothing about others' work**
- **Word choice should be accurate; you must let people grasp the core of the paper, with main and secondary clearly distinguished**

---

### Problem Formulation (Problem Definition)

1. First **describe the problem in words**, so that readers can understand
2. Then write **formulas** to formally define the problem to be solved
3. Finally, use **examples** to illustrate the problem, so that readers deeply understand

> A paper should best solve only one problem, not multiple problems. A good example is worth more than a good paper, and a carefully designed example running through the whole paper is crucial.

---

### Framework (Method Section) — The Second Most Core Part of the Paper

**Overall principle**: General-specific architecture; this chapter is a summary of all the technology in the paper, and each subsequent chapter also follows a general-specific structure.

1. **Overview of the overall architecture**: Introduce the overall Architecture of the proposed method/system; after reading this part, readers should basically understand the overall technology
2. **Workflow introduction**: Introduce the overall process of the system/method so that readers can easily understand, without being overly complex
3. **Each Component**:
   - Simple components are introduced directly and clearly in the Framework chapter
   - For complex components, clearly state what they do and the basic idea, and ref the detailed method to subsequent chapters

**Must achieve**:
- **Must clearly state novelty and contribution**
- **Must have an overall system architecture figure**
- **If there is no novelty, don't write it! The more you write about well-known content, the greater the side effects**
- Subsequent chapters: first write the points with strong novelty, then the ones with slightly weaker novelty, and finally the weakest ones
- Must be self-contained, well illustrated with figures, with leading text summarizing the content of each subsection

**Remember**:
- If a problem has multiple methods proposed, you need to be able to explain why not just propose the best method
- Mainly talk about your own things, put others' things into Related Work
- Symbols should be unified before and after, statements should be consistent before and after, and there should be no contradictions

---

### Experiments (Experiments Section) — The Third Most Core Part of the Paper

> The experiments section generally uses **past tense**, while other parts use present tense.

**Experimental setup**:
- First state the purpose of the experiment: what are we comparing?
- At least 3 datasets
- Evaluation methods, evaluation metrics (latency, Accuracy, cost, etc.)
- Baselines: must choose appropriate Baselines, **don't miss obvious Baselines**; if paper A has proven to be better than B and C, you may compare only with A and state in the text that A is better than B and C

**Overall comparison**:
- First write the end-to-end comparison, comprehensively comparing with all baselines
- First state what is being compared (latency/Accuracy/…), then analyze the effects shown in the experiment figures
- Give how much better, give the reason for being better, analyze in depth, and give findings

**Other detailed comparisons**: dataset size scalability, computing cluster scale-out, the impact of model size, etc.

**Ablation Study**:
- Evaluate the experimental effect of each contribution
- For example, three technical points A, B, C: compare All, without A, without B, without C — four methods
- Test different parameters

**Notes**:
- Must have real datasets, must have convincing Baselines
- Must highlight the conclusion: what did the experiments show? What did they prove? Why?

---

### Related Work (Related Work)

**Position choice**:
- Placed in **Chapter 2**: the paper depends on Related Work content (needs to use its framework, models, and methods)
- Placed in **the second-to-last chapter**: the paper does not depend on Related Work (without this part, the paper can still be understood). A good paper should generally be placed in the second-to-last chapter

**Writing norms**:
- **Do not plagiarize others' work; not a single sentence can be plagiarized; you must summarize and condense by yourself (red line!!!)**
- **Do not plagiarize others' sentences; not a single sentence is acceptable**
- Be truthful and factual; do not criticize others excessively; give others credit
- Grasp the key points; do not talk about minor details
- Be self-contained; clearly state the differences between this paper and related work, and why they cannot solve the problem of this paper
- Discuss by category; first state the method, then state the differences; for directly related papers, intuitively give their Limitations; for papers not directly related, criticize less and praise more
- Use conference abbreviations for references (SIGMOD/VLDB/ICDE/TKDE, etc.); go to DBLP to get the bibtex, including conference, pages, year, authors, and title

---

### Conclusion (Conclusion)

- Summarize the contributions of the paper; **do not repeat the Introduction**
- Introduction: the reader does not yet know the technical details → lead in the problem and method
- Conclusion: the reader has already read the paper → summarize contributions and significance
- Generally use present perfect tense

## Characteristics of a Good Paper / Characteristics of a Bad Paper

### Characteristics of a Good Paper

- Novel topic
- Strong technical depth
- Sufficient contributions
- Clear logical structure
- Good expression ability, concise and clear
- Well illustrated with figures
- Reliable / reproducible results
- Carefully selected references

### Characteristics of a Bad Paper

- **Delta work**: patching others' papers, insufficient novelty, insufficient contribution
- Others can come up with similar or better methods in 3-5 minutes
- Simple stacking of existing techniques (e.g., stacking multiple models, many parameters, mixing many methods)
- Even if related techniques haven't been used to solve this problem but have been used for similar problems, the contribution is still insufficient (e.g., someone else used MCTS to solve query rewriting, and you use MCTS to solve estimation problems—this will be questioned)
- **Remember: solving problems with old models or old methods; only the first one will be recognized and remembered**
- Minor patching on related techniques
- Methods that are too complex, hard to solve real problems, hard to deploy
- Confused logic, hard to read
- Small effect improvement
- Overly critical of other related methods
- Methods hard to reproduce, code and data not open-sourced

## Detailed Notes

- **Logic comes first**: every sentence should have a purpose; every two sentences should be connected; every claim should have evidence; every criticism should have grounds
- Must be **self-contained**: readers do not need any other paper background to understand
- Best to have an **example running through the whole paper**
- Each symbol has only one meaning; a symbol cannot be used for multiple things
- **Be consistent before and after**; no contradictions
- Consecutive sentences should be coherent; do not jump around
- Why can't existing methods solve these problems? Why can the method proposed in the paper solve the challenges? You must give concrete reasons
- Word choice must be idiomatic, and selection must be reasonable; an appropriate word makes it easier for people to understand
- Every sentence you write must have a purpose; do not pop out a sentence that makes people guess
- **Symbol issues**: use as few as possible; when there are more than 6 commonly used symbols, it's best to have a notation table; no ambiguity; concepts must be defined (where ** denotes **)
- `Figure~\ref{***}` uses `~` to prevent unwanted line breaks

## Paper Revision LaTeX Template

For submission revisions, the following standardized workflow is recommended:

**File organization structure**:
```
sections/
figs/
exps/
algs/
responses/
  meta.tex
  r1.tex
  r2.tex
  r3.tex
main.tex
commands.tex
LLM.bib
```

**Add to main.tex during Revision**:
```latex
\section*{Response to Reviewer Comments}

Dear Reviewers,

Thank you for your insightful reviews and constructive suggestions, based on which 
we have significantly improved our paper. The major revised parts are highlighted 
in \revision{blue} in the paper.

\input{response/r1}
\input{response/r2}
\input{response/r3}
```

**Commonly used commands**:
```latex
% Mark revisions in blue
\newcommand{\revision}[1]{\textcolor{blue}{#1}}

% Right-margin annotation (corresponding to reviewer comment number)
\marginpar[]{\revision{R1.W1}}{\revision{XXXXXX}}

% Left-margin annotation
\marginpar[\revision{R3.W2}]{}{\revision{XXX}}
```

Each response file (r1.tex, r2.tex, r3.tex) needs a summary table in the format: original reviewer comment number, revised content, revised section (e.g., R1.W1, clarify the motivation, section 1).
