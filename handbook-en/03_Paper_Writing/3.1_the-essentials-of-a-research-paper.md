# 3.1 The Essentials of Completing a Research Paper

Broadly speaking, the process of writing a paper is not merely a task of linguistic expression; it is even more so about constructing a clear logical framework and powerfully articulating your research ideas. Before you start writing, you need to think through the following important questions. Note that the items below are not the writing logic of a paper's Introduction, but rather a checklist that helps you reflect on whether the Idea and Solution are worth pursuing.

## 1. Background, Why & Problem: What exactly is this paper about? Why is it worth doing?

a. **Current State and Background**: First, explain what field your research is in [i.e., make your position clear from the outset by stating which (sub-)field your research belongs to]. What have the representative works in this field done? What commonalities and shortcomings exist across these studies?

b. **Limitations of Existing Methods**: In the research field you have chosen, what limitations do existing methods have? Are certain problems not fully solved? Or are existing methods unable to handle specific scenarios? Pointing out these limitations is how you justify the Motivation, significance, and value of your paper.

c. **Clearly Define the Problem**: Explicitly state the core problem that your paper seeks to solve. Generally, in this part of the introduction, you can clearly write "Our goal is to..." / "We study the problem of ...", directly stating the goal/problem of your research. Further characterize the intrinsic properties and hard constraints of this problem (e.g., scale, dynamism, heterogeneity, end-to-end overhead, etc.), so as to naturally derive the research goal or problem definition (Our Goal / Problem Formulation) that this paper aims to solve.

d. **Why: Why is this problem worth researching?**: Elaborate in detail on why this problem is worth researching, and what kind of academic value or practical application value solving this problem can bring. [Note, this part should correspond to the later How and So What sections], meaning that you are planting the motivation of the paper here, and the subsequent methods and contributions should well solve and respond to this motivation.

### Use a Running Example to Bring the Motivation to Life

When articulating the Why, it is strongly recommended to introduce a **Running Example** (a running example that runs throughout the paper). This is the key technique for upgrading your paper from a "dry technical report" to a "persuasive academic narrative."

**What is a Running Example?** It is a concrete, real application scenario that runs throughout the paper. You use it in the Introduction to lead into the problem, in the Problem Formulation to explain the formal definition, in the Methodology to demonstrate how your method works step by step, and in the Experiments to do a Case Study. It is the "anchor point" of your entire paper.

**Why do you need a Running Example?** Reviewers review a large number of papers every day, and they do not have the patience to read every paragraph of your paper carefully from beginning to end. A good Running Example can enable reviewers to "get" in 30 seconds what your problem is, why it is difficult, and why your method is clever. Abstract descriptions (such as "large models are prone to hallucination") are far less impactful than a concrete failure case.

**How to design a Running Example?** The most classic structure is the combination of "real scenario + failure case".

> For example: Suppose your research is on the reasoning capability problem of large models in Text-to-SQL tasks. Instead of just saying "LLMs do not perform well on complex queries", give a concrete example: a natural language question involving multi-table joins and nested subqueries, show that the SQL directly generated by the LLM is wrong (missing a critical JOIN condition), and then point out that this error causes the query result to include thousands of redundant records. This concrete failure case is far more effective than any abstract description at helping reviewers understand the urgency of your problem.

**Design Principles for a Running Example**:
- Real and concrete: derived from real data or scenarios; do not fabricate
- Concise but complete: simple enough for readers to quickly understand, yet containing enough complexity to demonstrate the difficulty of the problem
- Running throughout the paper: repeatedly appear in the Introduction, Methodology, and Experiments, forming a narrative closed loop

## 2. What: Why can't others do it, but you can? What are the technical challenges? What is the core idea?

a. **Technical Challenges**: You need to clearly articulate what specific research problem you are working on. For instance, if your research targets the problem that LLM Agents cannot automatically orchestrate good agent workflows for each task, and you want to propose a solution for automatic agent workflow orchestration, what are the technical challenges here? Why can't others do it? Is the algorithm complexity too high, is there a lack of data, or is there a lack of effective theoretical support, method modeling, or technical tooling? Technical challenges usually do not exceed 3 points, and you should explain why directly applying or simply extending existing methods is not effective. Common types of technical challenges include: explosion of search space, limited system overhead, difficulty of end-to-end closed loop, conflicts between theoretical/engineering constraints, etc. Generally, it is about the large search space leading to slow efficiency; the trade-off of jointly optimizing A and B is hard — is there a "kill two birds with one stone" solution? Using a naive solution directly yields poor accuracy/effectiveness, so we need a smarter method.

b. **Core Idea**: When facing these challenges, what new idea or method have you proposed? What inspired these ideas and methods? This part should be introduced in plain language, so that readers can get what you mean. For example, Inspired by... Observation (optional), we propose ...

## 3. How: How did you do it?

a. **Method Overview**: Briefly describe your method framework, providing a high-level overview.

b. **Method Details**: Describe in detail every key component and step of your method.

c. **Contribution Summary**: At the end of the Introduction, summarize your contributions (typically 3–4 points) in a clear list.

## 4. So What: You did so much, so what?

a. **Closed-loop Validation via Experimental Design**

b. **Main Experiments (Main Results)**: Conduct comprehensive comparisons against strong Baselines on standard Benchmarks, to prove that your method achieves significant improvements on the main metrics.

c. **Ablation Study**: For each key technical point you proposed in the "How" section, remove them one by one and observe the extent of performance degradation. This proves that every module you proposed is indispensable. An ablation study is essentially answering a refined version of "So What": what happens if this module is removed? For example, if you proposed two core techniques (a reasoning enhancement module and a self-supervised reward function), you need to remove them separately and show the performance drop.

d. **In-depth Analysis**: In addition to main metrics such as accuracy, multi-dimensional analysis must also be supplemented based on the hard constraints of the problem. The experimental design in this part must directly correspond to the Motivation raised in the Background/Why:

- **Case Study**: Return to the Running Example introduced in the Introduction, and show the complete working process and final result of your method on this concrete example. This is not only the perfect closure of the whole paper's narrative, but also the part most likely to make reviewers' eyes light up.
- Experiments should not only show "good performance" but also, through data, explain "why it is good" (Provide insights). Every experimental result should be followed by 1–2 sentences of analysis explaining the reasons for the performance differences.

---

> Please organize the content above carefully, discuss it thoroughly with your advisor, and decide whether to revise the paper's narrative framework above. Once no further revisions are needed, begin writing the paper. In the first stage of paper writing, I believe you should first draw the main figures of the paper well, and then write the text part of the paper. For example, drawing the Motivated Example figure well can better help you revise & check the writing of the Introduction chapter. Drawing the Solution Overview figure well can better help you clarify the structure and writing of the Methodology chapter.
