# 1.1 Preface and How to Evaluate the Quality of a Paper

## Preface

Many graduate students, upon entering their programs, hope to quickly publish a paper, yet they are unclear about what research is, what a paper is, and how to evaluate a paper. Naturally, such students take many detours — for example, working on an idea of no value.

## How to Evaluate the Quality of a Paper?

In the research process, we need to make a shift in mindset. Many students habitually evaluate paper quality from the perspective of how to write a paper; but for students just getting started, there is no need to prematurely fixate on how to evaluate a paper's long-term impact on the development of the discipline, because such evaluations typically require long-term academic accumulation and a deep understanding of the field.

In fact, the value of a paper is not only reflected in whether it can make an academic breakthrough, but also in whether it can be "read and understood" and accepted by other scholars and researchers. The purpose of a paper is to let others understand and gain value from it. Therefore, we can evaluate the quality of a paper from the reader's perspective (or the reviewer's perspective).

## Evaluating the Quality of a Paper from a Reviewer's Perspective

### What Reviewers Hope to See

1. **Novel Problem**: A new problem, especially defining a useful new problem.
2. **Novel Method**: A novel and effective method for solving an old or new problem.
3. **Nice Story**: Good writing, an engaging story, strong logic.
4. **Nice Presentation**: Beautiful typesetting, nice figures; avoid elementary grammar, typesetting, and spelling errors.

### What Reviewers Dislike Seeing

1. Old school problems, with a simple combination of existing methods
2. Even for new problems, it is not advisable to use existing methods that have already been employed within the DB community.
3. Worse Presentation: logic, writing, figures
4. Experiment: without strong baselines, inappropriate settings
