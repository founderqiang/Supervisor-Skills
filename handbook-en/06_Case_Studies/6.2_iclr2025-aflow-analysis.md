## The Thinking Model for Writing a Paper Introduction (the Introduction is a Condensed Version of the Entire Paper)

Generally speaking, the Introduction can be viewed as a "compressed version" of the entire paper: using the smallest amount of text to clearly describe the research object, thoroughly explain why the problem is difficult, and then make clear why our method is necessary. A clear writing organization is as follows: first, use a typical application scenario / running example to introduce the research background and needs; then summarize representative existing works, distilling the main limitations they reveal under key assumptions, data characteristics, workload and system constraints (usually no more than 3 points); on this basis, further characterize the essential properties and hard constraints of the problem (e.g., scale, dynamicity, heterogeneity, end-to-end overhead, correctness/consistency requirements, etc.), naturally leading to the research goal or problem definition this paper aims to address (Our Goal / Problem Formulation), or the core insight supporting the scheme design (Key Idea). Next, the key challenges to achieving this goal must be made clear (usually no more than 3 points), explaining why directly applying or simply extending existing methods is insufficient. Finally, give a methodology overview corresponding one-to-one to the challenges (overall framework and key modules), and conclude with the contribution points: problem definition/setting (if any), system/framework design, 1–2 key technical points, and sufficient experimental evaluation and analysis.

---

## Flowchart for Writing / Conceiving the Introduction

The core logic chain of the Flowchart:

> **Research Background** → **Research Frontier (Existing Methods)** → **Limitations (no more than 3 points)** → **Key Idea / Our Goal** → **Challenges (no more than 3 points)** → **Methodology Overview** → **Contribution Points**

| What is the research scenario? Why is it important? It needs a clear scenario definition + research motivation | "Large Language Models (LLMs) have emerged as powerful tools for solving complex tasks across various domains, including code generation, data analysis, decision-making, and question answering... However, the rapid advancement of LLMs heavily relies on manually designed agentic workflows – structured sequences of LLM invocations accompanied by detailed instructions. Designing and refining these workflows requires significant human effort, which limits the scalability and adaptability of LLMs to new, complex domains and hinders their ability to transfer skills across diverse tasks." **Writing idea**: set the broad background — LLMs are powerful, but their capabilities heavily depend on manually designed agentic workflows. This manual design process is time- and effort-consuming and limits scalability. |
| **Research Problem Analysis and Property Interpretation** | |
| Limitation 1 | Recent efforts have focused on automating the discovery of effective agentic workflows to reduce the reliance on human intervention (Khattab et al., 2024; Yüksekgönül et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2024). Despite these advancements, full automation has not been achieved. For instance, Khattab et al. (2024) require manual workflow setup before automated prompt optimization. Similarly, methods proposed by Yüksekgönül et al. (2024) and Zhuge et al. (2024) fail to capture the full diversity of workflows necessary for a wide range of tasks (Yu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024b; Sun et al., 2023), as their optimization objectives struggle to represent the breadth of possible workflows. **Writing idea**: although some works have begun to explore automated workflows, full automation has not been achieved. Existing methods still require substantial human intervention, or cannot cover sufficiently diverse workflow structures. |
| Limitation 2 | The inability to effectively model diverse workflow structures within these automated systems limits their utility and impact. ADAS (Hu et al., 2024) represents workflows using code, achieving a relatively complete representation. However, due to the efficiency limitations of its linear heuristic search algorithm, ADAS struggles to generate effective workflows within a limited number of iterations. **Writing idea**: even code-based representation methods (ADAS) suffer from search efficiency limits due to linear heuristic search, failing to generate effective workflows within a limited number of iterations. |
| Limitation 3 | This paper does not discuss a third limitation. The paper mainly targets two limitations — "low level of automation in workflow orchestration" and "weak ability to uniformly model diverse workflows" — as the motivations for AFlow. |
| **Discussion of the Paper's Novelty and Innovation Ideas** | |
| Key Idea | AFlow's core insight is to transform the design problem of agentic workflows into a **code-level search-optimization problem** — using MCTS (Monte Carlo Tree Search) to automatically search for the optimal workflow structure in the code space, thereby fully eliminating the need for human design. |
| Challenges | How to define a sufficiently expressive Search Space to cover various possible workflow structures? How to efficiently find the optimal solution in a vast search space? |
| Methodology Overview | **To address these challenges**, we propose AFlow, a Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS)-based framework designed to systematically explore and discover optimal agentic workflows. **AFlow represents workflows as flexible nodes connected by code-based edges, which encapsulate possible relationships such as logical flows, conditions, and dependencies.** These edges allow the workflow to be modeled as a graph or network, offering a powerful structure for capturing complex interactions between LLM invocations. **To enhance the search process and improve efficiency, AFlow introduces a novel concept of operators — predefined, reusable combinations of nodes representing common agentic operations (e.g., Ensemble, Review & Revise).** These operators serve as foundational building blocks for constructing workflows and are integrated into the search space, ensuring that the exploration process leverages known patterns of effective agentic operations. **AFlow** employs the MCTS algorithm to navigate this infinite search space. The framework's workflow optimization process incorporates several key innovations: a soft mixed-probability selection mechanism for node exploration, LLM-driven node expansion to introduce new possibilities, execution evaluation to assess workflow performance, and backpropagation of experience to refine future search iterations. **Writing idea**: the third technical point (MCTS) kills two birds with one stone — addressing both "slow search" and "bad search," making it the core innovation of AFlow. |
| Contribution Points | **We make the following key contributions:** **(1) Problem Formulation:** We formalize the workflow optimization problem, generalizing prior approaches as specific cases. This provides a unified framework for future research at both the node and workflow optimization levels. **(2) AFLOW:** We introduce AFLOW, an MCTS-based method that automatically discovers effective workflows across multiple domains with minimal human intervention. **(3) Extensive Evaluation:** We evaluate AFLOW on six benchmark datasets: HumanEval, MBPP, MATH, GSM8K, HotPotQA, and DROP. AFLOW outperforms manually designed methods by 5.7% and surpasses existing automated approaches by 19.5%. Notably, workflows generated by AFlow enable smaller LLMs to outperform larger models, offering better cost-performance efficiency, with significant implications for real-world applications. |
