# 4.3 Experimental Results Figure

## Core Objective

The mission of the Experimental Results Figure is to showcase your experimental data in the most intuitive and most persuasive way, supporting the core conclusions of the paper. A good experimental figure lets reviewers quickly verify whether your method is truly effective; a poor one makes reviewers question whether your experiments are reliable.

## Common Chart Types and Their Scenarios

Different experimental goals call for different chart types. The following are the chart types most commonly used in the data intelligence field and their applicable scenarios:

| Chart Type | Applicable Scenario | Design Key Points |
|---------|---------|---------|
| Grouped Bar Chart | Overall Performance comparison of multiple methods across multiple datasets | Highlight your method with a dark color and baselines with lighter colors. Leave appropriate spacing between groups of bars, and annotate the specific numerical values at the top of each bar |
| Line Chart | Parameter sensitivity analysis, training curves, trends with respect to time/scale | Use combinations of different colors + line styles + markers for different methods (such as solid line + circle, dashed line + square, dash-dot line + triangle), ensuring distinguishability even in black-and-white printing |
| Heatmap | Correlation matrices, attention weight visualizations, performance matrices of modules/methods | Use a continuous color scale (such as blue-white-red), and annotate the specific numerical value in each cell |
| Scatter Plot | Efficiency-Effectiveness Trade-off | X-axis is the efficiency metric (such as inference time), Y-axis is the effectiveness metric (such as accuracy). Your method should appear in the upper-right corner (fast and good) |
| Box Plot | Showing the distribution and stability of results | Suitable for presenting the distribution of results over multiple runs. More persuasive than merely reporting the mean |
| Radar Chart | Multi-dimensional comprehensive comparison | Suitable for comparing multiple methods across multiple dimensions (accuracy, efficiency, robustness, etc.) in a single figure |

## Design Principles

### Self-contained

Experimental figures must be able to stand alone. The caption, axis labels, and legend must be complete. Without reading the body text, readers should understand what this figure is comparing and what the conclusion is. The first sentence of the caption should summarize the core finding of the figure, for example:

> "Figure 5: Performance comparison on BIRD benchmark. Our method consistently outperforms all baselines across different model sizes."

### Scientifically Rigorous Visual Encoding

Avoid "Chartjunk". Do not use 3D bar charts, unnecessary shadows, flashy backgrounds, or overly complex grid lines. Maintain a flat, clean design. Colors and line styles must use **dual encoding**: considering that some readers may have color vision deficiency, or that the paper may be printed in black and white, you must never rely solely on color to distinguish different curves or bars.

### Highlight Your Method

If your method (Ours) performs best among multiple baselines, you can use a higher-contrast color (such as deep red or deep blue) to highlight the bars or curves representing your method, while setting other baseline methods to gray or lighter colors. In Alpha-SQL's Figure 1, "Ours" is highlighted with an eye-catching bright color, standing out prominently among the surrounding gray and light-colored baselines.

### Do Not Mislead the Reader

The Y-axis origin must be reasonable. If the accuracy of all methods falls between 60% and 80%, do not start the Y-axis at 0 (this would make the differences appear negligible), but also do not start it at 59% (this would exaggerate the differences). A reasonable practice is to start at 55% or 50% and indicate this in the figure.

## Case Analysis

**The problem of oversized canvases**: A common mistake is that the plotting Canvas is too large, causing text labels to appear relatively too small and illegible once inserted into LaTeX. The correct approach is to use a small plotting Canvas (such as 150×100pt in length and width), so that fonts naturally become larger after scaling.

> It is recommended to establish a unified plotting script template (such as `plot_utils.py`) at the early stage of the project, defining unified color schemes, font sizes, line widths, and other parameters. Then when drawing multiple experimental figures later, you only need to replace the data to keep the style consistent.
