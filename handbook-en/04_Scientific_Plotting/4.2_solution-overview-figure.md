# 4.2 Solution Overview Figure

## Core Objective

The Solution Overview Figure is the "soul" of the Methodology section of the paper. Its mission is to let readers (especially those reviewers who skip large amounts of text and go straight to the figures) quickly grasp your overall system architecture, core modules, and the data flow between them.

This figure typically appears at the beginning of the Methodology section (for example, right after the first paragraph of Section 3), and serves as the "map" for readers to understand your entire method. A good Overview Figure allows readers to maintain a global perspective while reading the subsequent technical details, always knowing where the module under discussion sits within the whole system.

## Common Design Paradigms

### Paradigm One: Pipeline / Workflow Figure

This is the most common paradigm. It applies when your method is a multi-stage processing flow, displayed from left to right (or top to bottom) in sequence: input → stage 1 → stage 2 → ... → output. Each stage is represented by a large box, and each large box may contain finer details of sub-modules.

**Case Study**: AFlow's (ICLR 2025) Figure 3 adopts a clear three-column layout: Search Space (left column, showing the definition of the search space, including the code representations of Node, Operator, and Edge) → Search via AFLOW (middle column, showing the four steps of the MCTS search process: Selection, Expansion, Evaluation, Backpropagation) → Search Result (right column, showing the optimal workflows found for different tasks). The logic of the entire figure is extraordinarily smooth, and readers understand it at a glance from left to right.

### Paradigm Two: System Architecture Figure

This applies when your method is a system that contains multiple interacting components. Use a large box to denote the system boundary, partition several sub-modules inside it, and use arrows to indicate the data flow and control flow between modules.

**Case Study**: Alpha-SQL's (ICML 2025) Figure 3 presents the overall architecture of the MCTS framework, including the LLM-as-Action-Model, the search tree's expansion process, and the self-supervised reward function's evaluation feedback loop. The data flow between modules is clear and unambiguous.

### Paradigm Three: Multi-level / Multi-perspective Figure (Offline–Online Module Figure)

This applies when your method involves multiple abstraction levels (such as macro to micro) or multiple perspectives (such as training stage and inference stage). You can use top-and-bottom stratification or left-and-right columns to present different levels / perspectives.

**Case Study**: LEAD's (VLDB 2026) Figure 3 adopts a top-and-bottom structure, showing what the overall framework does in Offline and Online respectively, as well as how the Online output interacts with the Training module. The top-and-bottom structure is generally used when the method consists of two large modules: Offline and Online, whereas left-and-right columns are commonly used for the linear flow of input-processing-output.

## Design Principles

- **Modularity**: represent each core component with a box, and subdivide further inside the box as needed.
- **Clear data flow**: use arrows to explicitly label the direction of data flow, avoiding crossings and clutter.
- **Distinct hierarchy**: distinguish components at different levels through color, size, and position.
- **Correspondence with the body text**: the name of each module in the figure must be consistent with the section headings in the body text.
- **Explicit input and output**: the most basic requirement is to let the reader know what the method's [input] and [output] are, and this must be clearly expressed in the figure.

> **Practical tip**: Regardless of the tool you use, before drawing the Overview Figure, it is strongly recommended to first hand-sketch a draft on paper and discuss with your advisor to confirm the layout and information hierarchy, then move to the computer to produce it. This avoids a great deal of rework. You can also refer to Chapter 5 Vibe Research to learn how to use AI to assist with plotting.
