# 4.1 Motivated Example Figure

## General Principles of Paper Figures

In top-tier conference papers in computer science and data intelligence (such as SIGMOD, VLDB, ICML, NeurIPS, etc.), the quality of figures and tables often directly determines a reviewer's first impression of the paper. When evaluating papers, "Nice Presentation" (professional typesetting and high-quality figures) is one of the core evaluation dimensions. **Producing a good figure may take 1-2 days, and this is an entirely normal investment.**

Before explaining specific figure types, let us first establish several universal **ironclad rules for plotting**:

| Ironclad Rule | Explanation |
|------|------|
| **Vector formats are mandatory** | When exporting and inserting figures, you must use PDF, EPS, or SVG formats. Absolutely do not use PNG or JPG screenshots inserted into the paper — they become blurry when zoomed in, which is the classic hallmark of an "amateur" |
| **Use reasonable font sizes** | Fonts in figures (especially axis ticks and labels) must, after being inserted into LaTeX two-column layout, be close in size to the body text (usually no smaller than 8pt). Never allow a situation where the text in the figure is so small that it requires a magnifying glass to read |
| **Keep the canvas size small** | In Matplotlib, set the canvas smaller (for example 150x100pt) so that fonts and lines naturally grow larger and thicker after scaling, producing a cleaner appearance. Do not use a large canvas paired with small fonts |
| **Colors must be colorblind-friendly** | Approximately 8% of males have color vision deficiency. Use colorblind-friendly palettes (such as ColorBrewer), and never rely on color alone to convey information |
| **Captions must not be omitted** | Every figure must have a complete caption so that the figure can be understood independently of the body text (Self-contained) |

This section focuses on the three most essential types of figures in papers.

---

## Core Objective

The Motivated Example Figure is **the single most important figure in the entire paper**. Its mission is to intuitively convey two core messages to the reader within 30 seconds after they finish reading the first two paragraphs of the Introduction: **"what problem this paper aims to solve"** and **"why existing methods fall short"**.

This figure usually appears in the upper-right of the first page or at the top of the second page, immediately following the paragraph in the Introduction that describes the Limitation. It is the "façade" that determines whether the reviewer will continue to read your paper seriously.

## Common Design Paradigms

In top-tier conference papers, the Motivated Example Figure primarily follows the three classic paradigms below:

### Paradigm One: Running Example + Failure Case (Most Recommended)

This is the most classic and most persuasive paradigm. Combined with the Running Example mentioned earlier, you present a real, concrete scenario and show the failure of existing methods in that scenario.

**Case Study**: Suppose your research is on the Text-to-SQL task. You can present in the figure a concrete natural language query (such as "Find all professors who published at least 3 papers in 2024 and their affiliated departments"), and then show side by side: (a) the incorrect SQL generated by existing methods (missing the GROUP BY clause) along with its erroneous query result; (b) the correct SQL and the expected result. Use red to highlight the erroneous parts, and green to highlight the correct parts.

### Paradigm Two: Existing vs. Ours Comparison Figure

Divide the figure into two columns (or two rows). The left side shows how existing methods work along with their limitations, and the right side shows how your method works along with its advantages. Through direct visual comparison, readers can see the difference at a glance.

**Case Study**: AFlow's (ICLR 2025) Figure 2 presents the concrete structures of the three core concepts Node, Operator, and Edge, allowing readers to intuitively understand the fundamental differences between this new workflow representation and traditional methods.

### Paradigm Three: Performance Teaser

Place a carefully designed performance comparison figure directly in the Introduction, so that at first glance the reader sees the effectiveness of your method. This paradigm is suitable when your method has a significant performance advantage.

## Design Principles

- Sketch on paper or a whiteboard first, discuss and confirm with your advisor, and then move to the computer to produce the figure. This avoids a great deal of rework.

## Recommended Tools

The Motivated Example Figure usually contains mixed elements such as text, icons, arrows, and code snippets, so it is recommended to draw it with **PowerPoint (PPT)** or **Figma**. PPT has the advantage of being quick to pick up and flexible in layout; once finished, export to PDF to obtain a vector figure. Figma has the advantage of convenient collaboration and component-based design. For figures that contain code snippets, you can first use a code-highlighting tool to generate a screenshot, and then lay it out in PPT/Figma.

Icons for illustrations can be downloaded from this site: [iconfont](https://www.iconfont.cn/).
