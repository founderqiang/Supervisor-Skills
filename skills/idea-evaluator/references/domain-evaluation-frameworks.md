# Evaluation frameworks for non-STEM research ideas

When an idea is not STEM or technical (no experiments, benchmarks, models,
or algorithms at its core), the Higher / Faster / Stronger / Cheaper /
Broader framework does not apply. Use the substitute dimensions below.
Classify by **research method**, never by department name: communication
research doing discourse analysis evaluates as humanities; communication
research running surveys evaluates as empirical social science.

## 1. Humanities and social sciences: five dimensions

Covers literature, history, philosophy, politics, sociology, education,
communication, art, and adjacent fields.

**1. Theoretical contribution.** What does the idea advance at the level of
theory: a new explanatory frame, a nontrivial extension or correction of an
existing one, or a fresh test of an old hypothesis on new material?
Scoring: 9-10, a genuinely new conceptual tool that could change how the
direction is discussed; 6-8, a nontrivial extension, correction, or
cross-domain transplant of existing theory; 3-5, existing theory applied to
new material with no advance in the theory itself; 1-2, no theoretical
lens, pure description.

**2. Methodological innovation.** Does the design see the material
differently: a new text-analysis strategy, a new archive combination, a new
cross-disciplinary lens, a new field method? Scoring: 9-10, a method design
that produces findings previously impossible; 6-8, deliberate method
choices well matched to the question; 3-5, routine method, no particular
design thought; 1-2, method mismatched to the question.

**3. Empirical discovery.** What previously unrecorded, unsystematized, or
unexplained phenomenon does it surface? First-hand documents, obscured
historical facts, and new patterns in qualitative material all count.
Scoring: 9-10, an important overlooked phenomenon with the potential to
shift the field's understanding; 6-8, systematizes a known but
underdocumented pattern; 3-5, confirms existing findings without new
information; 1-2, no empirical discovery, pure derivation.

**4. Explanatory power.** Can it explain more phenomena with fewer
assumptions: better coherence, fewer exceptions, sharper boundary
conditions than the standing account? Scoring: 9-10, unifies previously
competing explanations; 6-8, covers an important counterexample or boundary
condition the best current account misses; 3-5, on par with existing
accounts; 1-2, narrower than existing accounts.

**5. Social significance.** Does it carry actionable implications for
policy, practice, public discourse, or a specific community? Not mandatory,
but a bonus where present. Scoring: 9-10, directly speaks to a live policy
or public debate and could shift decision frames; 6-8, indirect but
identifiable implications for a practice field; 3-5, mainly theoretical
significance; 1-2, internal to academia.

Scoring discipline matches the STEM version: start at 5, add only with
grounds (material evidence or mechanism argument). The humanities form of a
mechanism argument is not an ablation; it is "this frame explains
counterexample X, which the rival frame cannot".

## 2. Humanities and social sciences: fatal-flaw substitutions

These replace their STEM counterparts:

1. **Insufficient engagement with the literature** (replaces "no novelty
   versus the closest work"). The field's core texts, founding studies, or
   recent central debates are not seriously engaged, making reviewers doubt
   the author knows the conversation.
2. **Research question cannot be operationalized** (replaces "unverifiable
   claim"). The question is too grand ("how does globalization shape
   culture") or too vague to design any study that answers it.
3. **Evidence does not match the claim** (replaces "baseline is not the
   real baseline"). The material (texts, cases, interviews, surveys) cannot
   carry the central claim; three cases claimed as a universal pattern.
4. **Method-paradigm conflict** (new). An empirical method for a
   speculative question (regressions to answer "what is beauty"), or a
   speculative method for an empirical one (theory in place of fieldwork to
   explain a community's behavior).
5. **Conceptual drift** (new). The core concept is never pinned down and
   shifts meaning between sections. Core concepts are the foundation of a
   humanities paper; an unstable one sinks everything on it.

The universal flaws remain in force: no compelling motivation, scope
sprawl, never discussing failure, data or ethics access, capability
mismatch.

## 3. Finance and economics: five dimensions

**1. Identification quality.** How credible is the causal identification:
what rules out reverse causality, omitted variables, selection? Scoring:
9-10, natural or quasi-experiment with a hard-to-challenge exogeneity
argument (policy discontinuity, geographic boundary, unexpected event);
6-8, IV or difference-in-differences with a reasonable but bounded
exogeneity case; 3-5, controlled regressions with no identification
discussion; 1-2, correlations presented as causal.

**2. Economic magnitude.** Statistical significance is not economic
significance: is the effect large enough to matter for policy or decisions?
Scoring: 9-10, large enough to move a policy debate or investment decision;
6-8, non-negligible economically; 3-5, present but small; 1-2, statistical
significance only, economic meaning unclear.

**3. Mechanism novelty.** Does it uncover a new transmission channel, not
merely a new correlation: why does A move B, through what? Scoring: 9-10, a
previously undocumented channel, decomposed via mediation analysis or a
structural model; 6-8, a new phenomenon explained through known channels;
3-5, documents a correlation with no mechanism discussion; 1-2, black-box
regression.

**4. Data contribution.** A new dataset, or a new stylized fact from
existing data: hand-collected microdata, first academic use of an
administrative dataset, a new cross-country panel. Scoring: 9-10, a dataset
that becomes infrastructure for the field; 6-8, first use of an
administrative or survey source for this question; 3-5, public datasets;
1-2, heavily mined classic data with nothing new.

**5. Policy relevance.** Can it tell a policymaker "doing X yields Y"?
Scoring: 9-10, directly answers a debated policy question with
identification strong enough to carry it; 6-8, indirect but identifiable
policy implications; 3-5, mainly academic; 1-2, unrelated to policy.

Discipline note: in this field, identification credibility outranks effect
size. A small, cleanly identified effect beats a large, endogenous one.

## 4. Finance and economics: fatal-flaw substitutions

1. **Endogeneity unaddressed** (replaces "unverifiable claim"). A causal
   claim with no credible strategy against reverse causality or omitted
   variables.
2. **Sample selection bias** (replaces "baseline is not the real
   baseline"). The sampling makes generalization impossible: listed firms
   standing in for "firms", one bank's customers for "consumers".
3. **p-hacking signals** (new). Only significant results reported; many
   specifications tried, the prettiest shown; no robustness checks.
4. **Missing robustness** (new). Core results never re-run with alternative
   measures, periods, or specifications. Reviewers expect at least two or
   three robustness checks.
5. **Canonical literature missing** (replaces "no novelty versus the
   closest work"). The founding papers or recent surveys of the direction
   are neither cited nor discussed.

## 5. Choosing the framework

Decide during the first-impression step, by method signals:

| Signal | Paradigm | Framework |
|---|---|---|
| experiments, benchmarks, ablations, model architectures | STEM / technical | original five dimensions (Higher / Faster / Stronger / Cheaper / Broader) |
| text analysis, archives, conceptual argument, discourse analysis | humanities | humanities five dimensions |
| surveys, interviews, statistics, case studies, fieldwork | empirical social science | humanities five dimensions, weighting empirical discovery and method innovation |
| regressions, IV, DID, RDD, panel data, financial variables | finance / economics | finance five dimensions |
| statutes, case numbers, precedents, doctrinal comparison | law | humanities five dimensions, weighting theoretical contribution and social significance |
| unclear | ask one question | "is the core method experiments, surveys, text analysis, or theoretical derivation?" |

Ideas that straddle paradigms ("NLP methods for political discourse")
classify by the **core contribution**: if the contribution is the NLP
method, STEM; if it is the political-science finding, humanities.
