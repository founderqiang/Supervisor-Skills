# Hedge calibration

A survey's credibility depends on every sentence's strength matching its
evidence. Overclaiming is spotted instantly by expert readers; underclaiming
drains the survey of judgment.

## Core principle: claim strength never exceeds evidence strength

## The ladder

### Strong (multiple independent studies agree; meta-analysis; large-scale validation)

- English: demonstrate, establish, show, confirm; consistently, robustly
- Chinese: 表明、证实、确立；一致地、稳健地

### Medium (two or three studies; bounded conditions)

- English: suggest, indicate, support the view that, be consistent with,
  point to
- Chinese: 提示、说明、支持……的看法、与……一致、指向

### Weak (one or two studies, or mechanism-only argument)

- English: may, might, could, appears to, seems likely
- Chinese: 可能、或许、似乎、看来

### No evidence

- English: to our knowledge, no study has examined; it remains an open
  question
- Chinese: 据检索结果，尚无研究考察过；仍是一个开放问题

## Common failures

### Overclaiming

- Wrong: "X proves Y" (prove belongs to mathematics)
- Wrong: "this is the best approach" (unqualified superlative)
- Wrong: "the first / the only / the optimal" without thorough verification
- Right: bound the claim: "among methods evaluated on dataset D, this
  achieves the highest score on metric M"

### Underclaiming

- Wrong: "it may possibly suggest that there could perhaps be..." (stacked
  hedges)
- Right: "the evidence suggests a moderate positive effect"

### Correlation written as causation

- Wrong: "X causes Y" without causal-inference evidence
- Right: "X is associated with Y"

## Do not stack jargon for authority

Authority comes from precise judgment matched to evidence, not from term
density.

- Wrong: "The paradigmatic shift in the methodological landscape
  necessitates..."
- Right: "Methods have changed substantially since 2023, and earlier
  conclusions may no longer hold."
